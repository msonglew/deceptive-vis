FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Feature Selection: (-2)

    • Only a single feature is visualized. Since it is not analyzed in relation to other features, the insight it proivdes lacks contextual grounding and does not reflect the full complexity of the entire data set.

  • Gradient: (-1)

    • California's data, which is an outlier for this feature, is included in the gradient. This causes the data from other states to cluster around similar color values, which makes differnce between them seem minimal and California stand out in contrast. In this case, California is an outlier with a low value.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Feature Selection: (-2)

  • Gradient: (-1)

Final Reflection

 When I first viewed the dataset and saw a row for each state, my initial instinct was to create a geographically-based visualization. I experimented with comparing pairs of features, but I could not formulate a clear question or find an effective way to visualize them, so I moved away from this approach. After deciding on a different direction, I began iteratively creating choropleths for features with valid numerical data types. I excluded features with string data types and focused only on numerical ones. This process resulted in about 15 visualizations. As I reviewed these visualizations, I looked for features that could be grouped together and that revealed contrasting patterns. This grouping was relatively straightforward. However, it was difficult to keep an open mind while also searching for a proposition with a built-in contradiction that could serve as the foundation for a meaningful question.

 Through this project, I have come to understand that ‘ethical analysis and visualization’ as walking a very fine line. A visualization should accurately represent the entirety of the dataset in context—otherwise, it risks misleading by presenting only a partial or decontextualized view. Misleading persuasive choices are those that do not give the reader context on the whole dataset and obscure its nuance. For example, instead of contrasting two separate figures, combining them into one visualization can more ethically show nuance—such as combining figure 1 and 2 shows that although California had the largest decrease in providers, it still has the most overall. Through my experience in this project, I have not been able to distinguish any ‘acceptable’ persuasive choices. There may be methods I haven’t yet explored that allow for more informative and ethically grounded persuasive storytelling.
